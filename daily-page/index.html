Lead Story

Good Friday Opens With Oil Still in Charge and Payrolls as the Next Real Test

Friday, April 3, 2026, begins with many major equity markets shut for Good Friday, leaving traders to carry Thursday's oil shock into a payrolls day with delayed price discovery. The cleanest current read is still that crude, the dollar, and inflation anxiety remain in control of the macro narrative.

The last full U.S. cash session, Thursday, April 2, ended with a late rebound in equities but without a full repair in the cross-asset message. U.S. crude futures settled near $111.63, Brent near $108.79, the dollar firmed, and gold and silver both fell, all of which still looked more inflationary than reassuring.

What changes on Friday is the calendar, not yet the market verdict. The March U.S. employment report is due at 8:30 a.m. Eastern Time on April 3, but with the NYSE and Nasdaq closed, the real equity response may be pushed into Monday, April 6. That makes this a transition day where macro information can hit before investors get a full cash-session reaction.

This is a low-liquidity, high-information day. Oil still matters more than the late Thursday bounce, and payrolls can reshape the Monday opening tone even if most of Friday's equity tape is missing.

Abnormal Moves

Oil Shock Reversed the Relief Trade

Thursday's 11.1% jump in USO remains the defining abnormal move. It erased the prior session's cooling-energy narrative and re-established crude as the market's first input.

Imported-Energy Stress Hit Asia First

The steep hit to South Korea and the weaker read across Japan and emerging-market proxies show how quickly higher oil feeds into import-sensitive economies.

Safe-Haven Logic Broke Down Again

Gold and silver falling alongside hotter oil remains one of the most useful cross-asset tells. It says inflation pressure and dollar strength are still overpowering the simple fear trade.

Special Situations

Globalstar / Amazon

Globalstar jumped 13.4% after a report that Amazon is in talks to acquire the satellite company. It matters because space and low-earth-orbit connectivity remain one of the clearest live strategic-M&A themes.

Blue Owl / Private Credit

Blue Owl capped withdrawals in two retail-focused funds after investors sought to redeem 40.7% of the technology-focused fund and 21.9% of the larger credit fund. That is financing-stress territory, not routine noise.

Airlines & Cruises

The second-order oil trade was immediate: Delta fell 1.2%, United 3.0%, Carnival 3.4%, and Royal Caribbean 3.0%. These are useful real-economy read-throughs for how fast the fuel shock is being repriced.
